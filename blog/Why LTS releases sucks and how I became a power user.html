  • I started using linux when, I was in high school, maybe a year before that, I mentioned it in a lot of places. As a go to distro, I installed ubuntu in my system which was being choked by windows for no reason. I don't hate windows, Almost everyone uses it, that's the reason I don't hate it, it is just not for me. I like to have greater control on the silicon I am using. So, I installed ubuntu, its plugin and play model was most convenient for me who was not aware of any internal intricacies of how a system works and even what are it's various components. As the time passed, I begin to take more interest in the Distro I am using, and so does the desire to tweak things according to my taste. The change occurred, and I had a bunch of gnome extensions installed in my DE. Everything was going great, the feeling of liberation from a shitty system to something productive had began to settle in when a pop up appeared saying this:- 
    There is an Upgrade to Ubuntu 20.04
  • And it was only after these messages which I encountered thrice throughout my time of using ubuntu, that made me say, "It sucks". As, soon as the upgrades were proceeded, there were things which would be breaking here and there. I am not sure if this is the experience with everyone else, but it was the case for me. Probably because I always used a system who's hardware is kind off old. But anyways, these upgrades will soon make some breaking changes which messed my installation and I had to do it all again. I was fed up with drivers not working, system updating issues, but what really hurt was the breaking of gnome extensions. Always, I mean always, there were some of my favourite extensions not working. Because the developer wouldn't have ported it to the upcoming release. So, after third time, I began to look for solutions for something better in linux world.
  • I those times, I could be easily found surfing on forums, asking for solutions rather than fixing things myself. The habit changed, slowly. Nonetheless, I came across a few threads saying fedora is better than ubuntu. There were often comparison posts between ubuntu, fedora and arch that you can find all over the internet. "It is difficult to install Arch" notion made me switch to fedora.
  • It is important to note here that apart from breaking extensions, I was also facing issues like firefox hiking CPU usage to maximum, system hanging sometimes, audio issues etc.
  • When I moved to fedora hoping for a better stable distro, I was not disappointed. The system was fast, there were few audio issues, but I fixed them (It is same time I developed my understanding about pulseaudio, wireplumber, Jack, ALSAand all other related terms). Gnome extensions used to break on upgrades, but now the system was stable. The other thing I loved about fedora is its package manager. Even after my ignorance towards understanding new concepts, dnf was something worth checking out. First of all, I didn't have to update and upgrade the system separately. Secondly, its representation of installing packages made it much more easy to understand what is being installed, what are the dependencies and other details. apt was pretty bad at this job. So much so, that just few months before changing to fedora I began using nala frontend for apt for better detail displaying.
  • Soon I understood that extensions were bound to break, I don't have control over it. What I have, is control over the display environment. Some, research showed that there are other desktop environments like Gnome. Deepin, KDE etc were good options. During this time , I became aware of the role of desktop environment, why do they exist, why are there different options, what is the role of login/display manger in all of this. I soon had a descent knowledge of how these components fitted together. I changed my display manger from gdm to sddm. I also installed deepin DE to test how it looked (even though I wanted to try KDE's apps). It looked great, but I didn't knew then and used it with the same user I was using gnome with. In a few days, the system became unusable and I reinstalled it again.
  • Search for Desktop environments brought me to wayland compositors. I understood how Xorg was used before it was replaced by wayland with Xwayland compatibility layer in most places. The compositors could be installed side by side with a DE. Though they are not fully blown environments, a lot of things like lock screen, notifications daemon, widgets, clipboard mangers etc needed to be installed and configured separately to be used with the compositor. What I liked most is the customizability of wayland compositors, Hyprland to be precise. S, I installed Hyprland and rest of my months were spent tinkering it to the right taste. Things like themes were inconsistent, but it was way fast and everything had a keybind for it. So, I could access anything without even touching my mouse. I became really fast. Almost every keybind was fit in my muscle memory. I started preferring TUI apps and command line for doing tasks and roaming around rather than relying on GUI apps and menus. That doesn't mean I didn't like GUI. In fact I understood Wayland inside out and then created a widget framework for it called Spell.
  • It would be again suitable at this time to tell that I ran a very stupid command from somewhere without knowing what it did. It removed all the users including the current from the sudo group. As a result, the system became unstable and I reinstalled everything. This incident moved me from just copy pasting commands at times to rather understand them and write things myself, that I fully know.
  • Everything was fine, just a singular issue remained. At times, when the system reached high CPU usage, Hyprland would hang so bad that I had to restart the system. Sometimes, that happened even when there were no significant programs running. I dug the log files, looked though the net and eventually landed on the culprit, or I should say the program which seemed like the culprit: systemd.
  • There were various posts ranting on systemd, bragging about how it didn't follow the UNIX philosophy. I didn't care. As long as my system is stable, I can use anything. But if it starts itching, I begin to scratch the problem out. Everything pointed towards some services causing problems, some minor errors here and there, some warnings and errors in journal logs. So, even not sure, I choose to switch to a non systemd distro. It was not because of all the public opinion out there. To me, systemd is very opaque, maybe I didn't looked hard enough but I had previously tried to fix my pulseaudio once and it didn't worked out well. The functioning of systemd were complex and I needed something simple.
  • So, I took a brave decision to install artix-plasma-runit. It is non systemd, I will make my Hyprland on apps of KDE and also the rolling release model fixed the pain caused by LTS. So, after fixing some dbus session activation issues, rebuilding Hyprland non systemd, and reinstalling my fav TUI apps, I was back. The hanging issues stopped too, and in all this distro hopping, Desktop env hopping, I learned a lot of things. I compiled code from source, fixed internal issues, created services and what not, enough for me to say that I became a power user.
  • So, I just want to end this blog with the fact that the journey was long and as you can see, there were various instances when I reinstalled my system, it is completely normal. Even if you don't become as obsessed as I am with your operating system, you should not stop trying and looking for new things.Things to fix, things to change. Am I transformed into a true power user, Idk. What I do know is that I do things way more efficiently than I did.
